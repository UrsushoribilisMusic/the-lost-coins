Chapter 10 - The Next Task
========================================

The transition from the sterile, hushed lab to the cold reality of the street was jarring. Lena stood on the university plaza, the Zug wind biting at her neck, and watched Erica disappear into the cafeteria.

She walked toward the edge of the campus, finding a pocket of privacy behind a concrete pillar. Her hands were steady, but her heart was hammering a frantic rhythm against her ribs. She pulled out her burner phone and dialed a number that didn’t exist in any directory.

Zheng picked up on the first ring. He didn’t say hello. He waited.

“The test is complete,” Lena said, her voice dropping into a low, professional hum. “The 9 BTC is yours. But that’s not why I’m calling.”

“Go on,” Zheng’s voice was a silky thread of anticipation.

“I’ve seen the lab. I’ve talked to the girl, Erica. It’s not just a theory, Zheng. She’s already doing it. They use ‘donated material’—cadavers—to map synaptic patterns. She told me the brain is like a book that’s been slammed shut. She has the tools to pry it open.”

Silence stretched over the line, heavy and predatory. When Zheng finally spoke, his voice sounded like he was tasting a vintage wine. “So Gonzales was right. The ice can be read.”

“Yes,” Lena said, her eyes tracking a group of students laughing nearby. They had no idea they were standing feet away from a woman discussing the digital resurrection of the dead. “She’s the key. But she’s not a mercenary. She’s an academic. She thinks she’s healing trauma, not looting vaults.”

“Then you make yourself indispensable to her,” Zheng commanded. “The hacker is useful, but the girl is the prize. Get closer, Lena. Become their best friend. Their benefactor. Find their pressure points. If they have a debt, buy it. If they have a dream, fund it. I want them so entangled in your life that they can’t breathe without your permission.”

Lena swallowed hard. The image of Erica’s amused wink in the cafe flashed through her mind—the warmth of it, the “funk.” She was being ordered to turn that heat into a trap.

“I understand,” Lena whispered.

“Good. Check your wallet,” Zheng said. “I’ve released the payments. Ten percent for the couple—consider it the bait. And a five percent finder’s fee for you. You’ve earned it, Lena. Don’t make me regret my generosity.”

The line went dead.

Lena pulled up her mobile wallet. The refresh icon spun for a second before the balance updated.

+0.908 BTC(Paul’s commission)

+0.454 BTC(Lena’s fee)

At current market rates, the numbers on the screen were a small fortune. Enough to pay her rent for a year. Enough to buy a very expensive black dress. But as she stared at the glowing digits, they didn’t feel like money.

They felt like blood.
