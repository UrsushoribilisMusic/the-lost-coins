Chapter 18 - The Whale Job
========================================

The setting for the meeting was a complete departure from the high-glamour of the Bürgenstock. Lena chose a quiet, dimly lit bar in the backstreets of Zug—the kind of place where the wood is dark, the booths are deep, and the patrons mind their own business.

Paul looked better than he had in days, though he still had that restless energy of a man whose bank account was a leaking bucket. He sat across from Lena, his eyes scanning her face as if looking for the hidden fine print.

“The client is impressed, Paul,” Lena began, her voice a low, steady purr. “So impressed that they want to accelerate the timeline for the ‘Whale.’ But the parameters have shifted.”

Paul leaned in, his drink forgotten. “Shifted how?”

“It’s not a hard drive this time,” Lena said, dropping the bomb with surgical precision. “The data is locked in a wet-ware environment. We’re talking about a recovery from a preserved biological source. A brain, Paul. Specifically, a cryopreserved one.”

Paul went still. The mechanical whir of his hacker mind seemed to stutter. “You’re talking about Erica’s field. You want her to extract a key from a dead man?”

“I want her to use the tech she’s already perfected,” Lena countered. “She maps the dead for the university for peanuts. My client is offering a fully-funded research trip to California. They’re sourcing a mirror of her lab in Zug—top-of-the-line Shenzhen replicas—and staging it in a private facility near the Sequoia National Forest. No university oversight, no red tape. Just pure science.”

She paused, letting the silence hang.

“And the commission?” Paul asked, his voice barely a whisper.

“It’s an O.G. wallet. Over a thousand Bitcoin, Paul. Even at the current dip, your ten percent is more than you’d raise in five years of VC rounds. It’s the end of the struggle. For both of you.”

Paul looked down at his hands. He thought about the red lines on his trading screen, the overdue server bills, and the way Erica had talked about the Sequoia giants during their dinner in Lucerne. She had a bucket list—and those ancient trees were at the top of it.

“California,” Paul murmured. “She’s always wanted to see the Sequoias. She’s obsessed with the idea of trees that have lived for two thousand years.”

“Then give her the trees,” Lena said, reaching across the table to touch his hand. The “funk” was there, but it felt colder now—more like a tether. “Tell her a private donor wants to fund a breakthrough study. Tell her she gets to be the first to pull a digital fortune out of the ‘ice.’ She makes the list of every piece of equipment she needs, and the client makes it happen.”

Paul took a long breath, the weight of the decision pressing into his chest. “I’ll talk to her. If she’s in, I’m in.”

“Don’t take too long,” Lena said, sliding a napkin across the table. On it, she had written a single number:1,000+ BTC. “The client is already moving the ‘hardware’ to the forest. The window is opening.”

As Paul left the bar, his mind was already a blur of logistics and dreams. He didn’t see the black sedan pull out of the shadows half a block away, its headlights off, following him like a shark in dark water.
