Chapter 50: The Hamlet in Zug

The afternoon sun glinted off the pristine surface of Lake Zug, casting a peaceful, silvery light over the cobblestone streets of the old town. Four months had passed since the "Big Fire" in the Sequoias, and the air here smelled of crisp Alpine wind and roasting coffee—a world away from the scent of cordite and damp earth.

Erica and Lena walked side by side, their pace slow and synchronized. Both were beginning to show, their silhouettes softened by the early stages of a shared future. They stopped in front of a boutique window filled with hand-knitted woolens and wooden toys.

"What do you think of 'Matthias'?" Lena asked, holding up a tiny, cream-colored cardigan. "It feels... sturdy. Grounded."

Erica smiled, touching a soft pair of leather booties. "It's a good name. Paul likes 'Finn,' but I told him that might be a bit too much on the nose, given everything. We're leaning toward 'Julian.' It sounds like someone who would actually study for his exams instead of hacking the global economy."

They laughed, a sound that came easily now. The trauma of the mill hadn't vanished, but it had been paved over by the mundane, beautiful logistics of new life.

The relocation to Switzerland had been Alcor's doing. Realizing the brilliance—and the sheer resilience—of the team, the foundation had offered Paul a senior position in their European research unit. They didn't want him mapping "Whales" anymore; they wanted him building the ethical frameworks for the future of cryo-preservation. It was clean work, well-funded, and, most importantly, safe.

"Paul called me twice while we were in the pharmacy," Lena said, tucking a stray hair behind her ear. "He's obsessed with the floor plans for the farmhouse. He thinks the barn can be converted into a lab for him and Chen."

"The 'Panda Sanctuary,'" Erica joked, nodding.

They had found a small, stone-walled farmhouse in a quiet hamlet just outside the city. It sat on a rise overlooking the valley, surrounded by green pastures and the distant, rhythmic clanging of cowbells. It was large enough for all of them—a place where they could grow together, navigating the complicated, non-traditional family they had become. There was a room for Erica's baby, a room for Lena's, and a wide, sun-drenched porch where they could sit and watch the seasons change.

"He found a local craftsman to build the cribs," Erica continued. "Solid oak. He wants them to be identical. He says he doesn't want the kids fighting over who has the better setup before they can even crawl."

"He's going to be a good father," Lena said softly, her gaze drifting toward the mountains. "To both of them."

"He's had a lot of practice being the glue that holds things together," Erica agreed. She looked at Lena, the old professional rivalry and the sting of the Napa night finally settled into a quiet, mutual respect. "We all have."

As they turned the corner toward the cafe where Paul and Chen were waiting, Erica felt a familiar flutter—a small, persistent kick. She reached out and squeezed Lena's hand.

The "Big Fire" was a legend now, a file in a federal cabinet and a story told in hushed tones in the crypto-underworld. But here, in the quiet heart of Switzerland, the only thing that mattered was the price of timber, the warmth of the sun, and the two heartbeats that were about to change their world forever.
