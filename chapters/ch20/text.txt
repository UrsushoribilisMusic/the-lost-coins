Chapter 20 - The Ethical Barrier
========================================

The sunlight in their Zug apartment was unforgiving, highlighting the dust on Paul's unused server racks and the stack of "Final Notice" bills on the kitchen island. Paul stood by the espresso machine, his heart racing as he laid out the proposal Lena had presented in the bar.

"A thousand Bitcoin, Erica. Minimum," Paul said, his voice hushed with a desperate sort of reverence. "The client is a private donor. A full research sabbatical in the Sequoias. They're building you a mirror of the HSLU lab in a mobile unit. Everything you need, sourced and ready."

Erica sat at the small wooden table, her hands wrapped around a mug of tea. She didn't look excited. She looked analytical.

"And the 'subject'?" she asked. "Lena mentioned a wet-ware environment. We're talking about a human brain, Paul. A preserved one. Who is it?"

"She didn't say. Just that it's a high-priority recovery for a very wealthy estate," Paul pushed, leaning against the counter. "Someone who wanted their data retrieved post-mortem. It's exactly what your research is for, Erica. It's the ultimate proof of concept."

Erica set her mug down with a deliberate clack. "It's a legal minefield, Paul. I don't care how many zeros are in the commission. I don't 'just go' to a forest in California to perform an off-book procedure on a human specimen. There are protocols. Institutional Review Board approvals. International transport permits for biological tissue."

"Lena said the donor is handling the logistics—"

"I don't care what Lena says," Erica interrupted, her eyes sharp. "I need to see the paperwork. I want the ethical clearance forms, the medical transport manifests, and the donor's consent documentation. If we are moving a preserved brain across state lines or working on it outside a certified facility, I need to know it's above board. I'm a researcher, Paul, not a digital grave robber."

Paul flinched. The word grave robber felt like a cold splash of water. "Erica, this is the win we've been waiting for. It saves the company—"

"Then it can afford to be legal," Erica countered. She stood up, her posture rigid. "Tell Lena I'm interested in the science, but I won't move a single electrode until I see the paper trail. I need to know this specimen was legally obtained and that the extraction is authorized. No papers, no trip."

She walked into the bedroom, leaving Paul alone with the hum of the refrigerator and the weight of a request he knew Lena couldn't easily fulfill.

Paul pulled out his phone. He was walking a tightrope. He had to convince Lena to provide a reality that Erica would believe, or the whole sixty-million-dollar dream would evaporate before they even left Switzerland.
